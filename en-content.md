# OUR STORY

## Meta

## Section 1: Argentinian beef, from the source (400 words)

**Opening quote (display):**
"You can taste the difference. You can always taste it."

**Body:**

In Argentina, they don't drink coffee the way we do. They drink mate, from a gourd, for hours, passed around a circle. And they don't eat steak the way we do either. They eat asado. What we think of as "a piece of meat on a plate" is, for them, an entire afternoon ritual. A family affair. A reason to keep a Sunday free, light the fire early, and not sit down to eat until everyone has arrived.

That entire ritual, that entire way of thinking about food, is Amigo. It is why we call ourselves an authentic Argentinian restaurant in Amsterdam, and not a steakhouse that happens to serve some Argentinian dishes.

When we opened our doors on the Prinsengracht in 2011, we had one rule we would never bend: the beef must come from Argentina. Not through a wholesale network where the origin of a cut becomes a shrug. Not through intermediaries optimizing for margins. Directly from our trusted Argentinian importer, who sources from the Pampas, where the cattle roam on unlimited grass, eat calmly, receive no growth hormones, and get the time to become what a cow is supposed to become.

A new shipment arrives every week. Bife de lomo (the tenderloin, the most tender cut on the animal). Bife de chorizo (Argentinian sirloin, deep in flavor). Bife ancho (rib-eye, well marbled). Cuadril (picanha, with that unmistakable fat cap). Dry-aged where appropriate, held at the right temperature, ready for the parrilla the moment you sit down.

We've worked with the same importer for so many years that we know each other like family. If something isn't right in a shipment, we work it out. If a beautiful new batch arrives, we see it first. That relationship isn't marketing copy. It's simply how it works when you take seriously what ends up on the plate.

You can taste the difference the first time. Argentinian beef has a different texture, a deeper umami, more character. Not because we do anything special in the kitchen. Because the cattle got something special: space, grass, time.

At Amigo, we then let the beef do its own work. No marinade that overwhelms. No sauces that hide what the kitchen can't handle. Only heat, salt, and time. Chimichurri at the table if you want it.

**Right quote, Playfair italic:**
"Good steak needs no help. Only heat, salt, and time."

## Section 2: The tableside grill philosophy (320 words)

**Subheading:** The parrilla at your table

**Body:**

When you order a steak with us, you don't get it on a plate. You get a small parrilla at your table, with glowing coals, and your meat resting above the heat. Every bite you take, you can hear the next one gently sizzling beside it.

That's not a gimmick. That's how Argentinians have been eating for generations.

In Argentina, everyone cooks this way. In the backyard at home, in a restaurant, on the street during a football match. The parrilla is the center of the gathering. Everyone sits around it, the meat rests above it, and the evening unfolds on its own. Nobody looks at a phone. Everyone looks at the fire.

What we've adopted is not the self-grill model where you wrestle with tongs as a guest. Your beef is already perfectly cooked on our kitchen grill, exactly to the doneness you asked for. The tableside parrilla keeps it at temperature. No one has to rush. Your bife de lomo stays pink and warm until you pick it up, even if you want to keep talking first, about the new house, the trip, the son who is suddenly taller than you.

This is why people stay longer with us than average. Why two-hour reservations rarely feel like enough. Why we have so many regulars who have been coming back for more than a decade, who say: "an evening at Amigo actually feels like an evening."

It is also why some Dutch steakhouses don't dare copy this. It takes more space per table. It asks more of the service. It isn't efficient. But dining at the table isn't supposed to be efficient. It's supposed to be slow, warm, and personal.

**Quote, Playfair italic:**
"Nobody wants a cold steak. So we put a fire next to the plate. Simple solution, old idea."

## Section 3: On the Prinsengracht since 2011 (320 words)

**Subheading:** A place that belongs to the city

**Body:**

Prinsengracht 188. Three minutes' walk from the Anne Frank House. A stone's throw from the Westerkerk. On one of Amsterdam's most beautiful canals, in a building that has housed a restaurant for generations. The floor tilts a little, the walls are old, and that is exactly how it should feel.

We arrived here in 2011, and stayed.

We had a second location on the Rozengracht for a while. We let it go in 2024. Not because business was bad, but because we learned that one good place is better than two decent ones. On the Prinsengracht, we can give the attention we want to give. Every guest, every steak, every detail. A table isn't a sales slot on a sheet. It is an evening in someone's life.

What makes the Prinsengracht special for a restaurant is that it offers everything people come to Amsterdam for, without ever feeling like a tourist trap. No thousand-guests-a-day flow. Just a neighborhood where locals still live, expats work, and travelers walk to because they are looking for something real. The canal belt was, centuries ago, where the merchants of the Golden Age built their homes. We now sit in one of those same buildings, serving food from another continent, and somehow it feels like we belong.

We're open daily from noon to midnight. Fridays and Saturdays until 1 AM. Our terrace opens in mid-April, weather permitting, and inside you look out onto the canal and the Westerkerk through large windows all year round.

In the years we've been here, we've collected Travelers' Choice awards from Tripadvisor, sit around position 158 out of more than 5,000 Amsterdam restaurants, 8.8 on TheFork, 96% recommended on Facebook. But that's not what we watch. What we really track is how often the same faces come back. And that number keeps growing.

For anyone searching for an authentic Argentinian restaurant in Amsterdam, a real parrilla evening, or simply the best bife de lomo in the city: this is where we've done it since 2011.

"We don't need the press. We need you back."

Picture this: you're sitting at a table with glowing coals and a bife de lomo that just came off the kitchen grill. To your left, the Westerkerk, that tall pointed tower where the bells ring every quarter hour. To your right, a row of canal houses that have stood there for four hundred years. This is what canal side dining in Amsterdam actually feels like. Not the idea of it. The real thing.

Our terrace at Prinsengracht 188 is one of the few places in the canal belt where you can eat right on the water without being swept up in a tourist merry-go-round. It opens in mid-April, weather permitting, and runs until the end of October. Attached to the restaurant, but it feels like its own small room. About eight tables, all with a view. Summer evenings book up fast, especially on weekends, so reservations are worth it.

Indoors, you get canal views year-round through large windows that span the facade. Winter on the Prinsengracht is a different kind of beautiful: lights reflected in the water, the Christmas market just around the corner, and a parrilla at your table that keeps the plate hot and your hands nearby. Not less. Different.

## Getting here (SEO: Amsterdam centre, Anne Frank House)

### By public transport

From Central Station, it's a 15-minute walk. By tram (lines 13, 17), get off at Westermarkt stop, then 2 minutes on foot. The night tram line 13 runs until 00:30.

### By car

The Prinsengracht is in the canal belt, so paid parking everywhere. Parking garage Q-Park De Kolk on Nieuwezijds Kolk is about 5 minutes on foot, with 200+ spaces. Expect €8 to €12 per hour in the centre.

### By bike

Amsterdam is a bike city, and you can park along the canal near us. Summer evenings the bike racks fill up, and bikes get parked across the bridge.

### From the Anne Frank House

3 minutes' walk. Head onto the Westermarkt, cross over, and you're on the Prinsengracht. We're on the left side, number 188.

## Best time to visit

For a quiet lunch: weekdays between 12:00 and 14:00. For an unhurried evening: Sunday through Thursday. For a festive atmosphere: Friday or Saturday, book 1-2 weeks ahead.
